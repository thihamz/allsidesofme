CURATORIAL NOTE
All Sides of Me is an encounter with material and meaning, exploring aspects of personhood and the complex, ever-evolving biological and digital systems that inform our views of self. We all see the world through individual experience, spatial, social, political and conceptual. We can discover new realities and memories once we acknowledge the importance of varied and diverse engagement, this being a driver in how we navigate our environment. Before technology, measures of pleasurability were aligned with significant non-technological sensory experiences, such as new tastes, smells, visual stimulus and experiences with touch. Looking at the importance of sensory encounters allows for accessible experience, in particular being inclusive of the diversity of different ways to observe.

Being a part of natural and transformative spaces allows for the exploration of interspecies relationships as well, and the collaborations of human and non-human life forms through the absence of language. Most of nature communicates in exchange of scent, and chemical production/ release, creating an alternative sensual landscape to what we perceive through a human centred lens.

Other parts of experience are represented in landscape and place, and how this helps examine the biographies of a person, inclusive of their environmental experiences outside of themselves. When people find themselves reflected within specific communities, places, aesthetics, movements, tastes, and scents, it influences what they see in other objects, and spaces. The interpretations of senses change depending on what surrounds them, and calls into question their underlying associations; these associations merge as part of the spectator’s past, present and future.

We encourage all visitors to engage with the work presented, through any way that feels comfortable and rewarding, we hope that within this exhibition there is something that feels representative of you, your interests and the plentiful and various sides of yourself.

Mathilde Wittock
Website | Instagram
Sound is a fundamental aspect of the world we live in, as everything vibrates and emits frequencies. Scientific research has shown that sound frequencies can influence our cells and their functions on a cellular level. In today’s world, the sounds we hear are mostly artificial and often overpower natural sounds. Despite being invisible to the human eye, man-made soundwaves have significant effects on both the micro and macroscopic levels of our organisms.
What if we could use sound frequencies to engineer material growth that could help counteract these effects?
Enter Soundwall, an acoustic project that combines designer expertise with biology, physics and arts. The project involves growing soundproofing tiles made from fast-growing and carbon-hungry grass seeds, creating a high-density root foam material that absorbs mid to high-frequency ranges of sound. The resulting wall of tiles is designed with patterns that represent the frequencies of the audible sounds that surround us.
Soundwall challenges our current production system by bringing back nature and designer 'savoir-faire' at the center of our economic system, creating regenerative and acoustic solutions for indoor architecture. By exposing the invisible world of soundwaves, Soundwall inspires new ways of using frequencies for positive impact, creating hope for a more sustainable future.

I’ve consistently obsessed over so many subjects in the past when it comes to my art: from technology to nature, philosophy to religion, and cooking to gardening. I tend to imagine parallel worlds or outer space, but where our variation of reality would still exist in these alienated spaces. Trying to understand species other than homo sapiens: their biologies, working mechanisms, and systems. Fundamental physical and metaphorical elements taken from ancient wisdom, such as Confucianism, bagua, medicine, mathematics, horoscopes, and astrophysics. Viewing or relating our species's evolution, revolution, or development, from looking at the past and attempting to calculate the future. I’ve attempted to befriend and understand nature by finding ways to connect to the original source of energy for all living/non-living things that are currently cohabiting on the planet. Where a lot of my work is time durational and collaborates with other life forms, in which they have their own freedom and consciousness for their own chosen direction. My practice is undergoing a constant metamorphosis, where stimulating chaotic thoughts enrich my work with deeper, funny connections and understandings.I like to think of the 'functioning' worlds or systems I forge with each work, the fiction I weave, and how idiosyncrasies engage with the fantastical. There are no determined starting or ending points in what I make, as I want the experiences to be carried on by whoever comes across them, by any life forms.

Jack West
Instagram
West explores objects and materials within the studio and translates these into the virtual environment resulting in a practice that centres around sculpture and video. The process of creating objects within a virtual space is entirely removed from the hand and would intuitively appear as to be a process that is polarised to the more traditional ways of making that make up a sculptural practice – be it the turning of a bit to create a hole in metal, or the shaving and refining of wood with a plane to flatten and remove signs of it’s natural form. The idea of ‘the machine’ continues as a central theme in his practice. A machine is ultimately an object created to remove man from the need to labour. And so, through its repetitive, unthinking actions this mechanical functioning acquires an apparent purposefulness – it transforms these blind movements into acts of ritual. Parts are created that appear to have purpose but are then repeatedly stacked in way that beguile any useful function. Questioning ideas of purpose and function and exploring the roles of work and labour in our society. Visitors are not encouraged to touch this artwork

Lucia Giron
Instagram
Turning to the biological alchemy that supports almost all life on earth, ‘Like a Leaf’ integrates the building blocks of photosynthesis [air, water, and light], as tools for design.
Cyanobacteria captures carbon from the atmosphere and transforms it into a binding mineral through the process of photosynthetic biomineralization, creating a living, breathing, carbon-hungry material with enhanced photosynthetic ability.
Here material must be treated not like a brick but like a leaf, shifting towards a way of building that blends with the spheres of the earth.
Happy for visitors to gently touch the sample material but not the actual work
